The pitch for every AI tool is speed: ship faster, build faster, launch faster. True. But nobody mentions the catch — *speed is a multiplier, and a multiplier amplifies whatever you feed it.* Point it at good judgment and you're unstoppable. Point it at no judgment and you're now generating mediocrity faster than anyone in history.

## The trap

It feels like progress. You're producing five landing pages a day instead of one. The dashboard of "things made" goes vertical. But if all five are generic, you didn't get five times more valuable — you got five times more *output*, which is not the same thing and is sometimes worse. Now there's more to maintain, more to wade through, more average stuff with your name on it.

Volume was never the bottleneck. The bottleneck was always knowing which thing was worth making and whether it came out good. AI obliterated the first constraint and left the second one fully intact — and now glaringly exposed.

## Why this is good news (if you have taste)

When everyone speeds up, the differentiator stops being "how fast can you produce" and becomes "is what you produce any good." That's a *harder* skill to fake and a rarer one to have. The people who paired AI's speed with real judgment just became dramatically more valuable. The people who used it to skip developing judgment just made more slop, more confidently.

The market will sort this out fast. A feed full of AI-generated everything makes the genuinely good stuff stand out *more*, not less. Scarcity moved.

## What to actually do

Slow down at the only step that matters: the decision. Spend the time AI saved you on *judgment* — which version is right, is this actually good, what should I cut, would a senior person defend this? Use the speed to explore more options, then use taste to kill all but the best one. Speed to generate, taste to select.

**The takeaway:** AI didn't make taste optional. It made taste the *entire* job, because everything around it got automated. Faster slop is still slop. Aim the multiplier carefully.
