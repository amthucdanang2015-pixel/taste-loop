A founder messages me: "Users sign up and don't come back. Can you redesign it?" Nine times out of ten, the redesign is a way to avoid a scarier question — *do people actually want this?* A prettier version of something nobody wants is still something nobody wants.

This is uncomfortable, so let's make it useful.

## The tell

Design problems and PMF problems produce different data.

- **Design problem:** people *try* to use it and get confused, frustrated, or lost. They want the outcome; the interface is in the way. You'll see rage-clicks, drop-off at a specific confusing step, support questions about *how*.
- **PMF problem:** people understand it fine and just… don't care. They sign up, look around, leave, and never think about it again. No confusion. No questions. No pull. Flat retention from day one.

If users are confused, design helps. If users are indifferent, design is lipstick.

## Why founders pick the design story

Because it's safer. A redesign is a concrete, fundable, do-able task that *feels* like progress and doesn't require admitting the core idea might be off. PMF work is murkier and threatens the whole premise. So we reach for the paintbrush.

I get it — and sometimes design genuinely is the problem. But you owe yourself the honest diagnosis first.

## The 20-minute test

Talk to five people who churned. Not a survey — a call. Ask one question: "What were you hoping this would do for you, and why'd you stop?" If they describe friction, you have a design problem and it's fixable fast. If they shrug and say "it was fine, I just didn't need it" — no redesign on earth fixes that. You need a sharper wedge, a different user, or a different problem.

**The takeaway:** design makes a wanted thing lovable. It can't make an unwanted thing wanted. Diagnose before you decorate.
