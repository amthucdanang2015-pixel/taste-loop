There's one background that instantly dates an app as "untouched AI output": the purple-to-blue diagonal gradient. Not because gradients are bad — because *that specific one* is the path of least resistance every tool reaches for, so it's now on ten thousand identical landing pages. It's a tell. Here's what to use instead.

## Why it reads as generated

The default gradient is static, high-saturation, and diagonal — three properties that, combined, scream "I accepted the first output." Real designers either commit to flat confidence or add motion and texture so the gradient feels alive. The lazy middle — a still, loud diagonal — is the giveaway.

## Three backgrounds that read as designed

**1. A slow-moving gradient mesh with grain.** Take a gradient and (a) make it *drift* slowly on a 15–20s loop, and (b) overlay subtle SVG noise. Movement and texture transform the exact same colors from "default" to "crafted." Prompt it: *"animated multi-stop gradient mesh that morphs slowly over 20s, overlaid with low-opacity film grain."*

**2. Flat color with one confident accent.** The opposite move: kill the gradient entirely. A single, slightly-off neutral (warm charcoal, deep ink, bone white) with one restrained accent color reads as expensive precisely because it's not trying. Confidence is a look.

**3. A cursor-reactive spotlight on near-black.** A dark canvas where a soft light follows the cursor, revealing a faint grid and barely-there gradient text. It's interactive, it's premium, and it's the opposite of a static diagonal. (It's effect #10 in the [showcase](/showcase) if you want the prompt.)

## The principle

A background should do one of two things: get out of the way (flat, confident) or be *alive* (moving, textured, reactive). The default gradient does neither — it just sits there being loud. Pick a lane.

**The takeaway:** the colors aren't the problem. Static, loud, and untouched is the problem. Add motion, add texture, or strip it down — any of the three beats the diagonal everyone's seen.
