The takes write themselves: "AI means anyone can build, designers and engineers are cooked." It's backwards. When the cost of *making* something drops to near zero, the value doesn't disappear — it relocates. It moves to the one thing that didn't get cheaper: **judgment.**

## The bottleneck always moves

Every time production gets easier, the scarce skill shifts up a level.

- When publishing got free, *writing* stopped being the bottleneck — *being worth reading* did.
- When cameras got into every pocket, *taking a photo* stopped mattering — *having an eye* did.
- Now that building software is a prompt away, *shipping code* stops being the moat — *knowing what to ship and what "good" looks like* becomes everything.

The supply of "things that technically work" just went vertical. The supply of taste did not. Basic economics tells you which one gets valuable.

## What "taste" actually means here

It's not vibes. It's a stack of specific, learnable judgments:

- Knowing the default output is the *starting point*, not the answer.
- Feeling when motion is 50ms too slow or a hierarchy is one level too flat.
- Knowing which feature to cut so the product means something.
- Hearing a user describe a problem and knowing which 10% to actually build.

AI can generate a thousand options. It can't tell you which one is right for *this* user, *this* moment, *this* brand. That selection is the job now.

## The move

Stop competing on "I can build it." Everyone can build it. Compete on "I know which version is worth building, and I can tell the difference between fine and exceptional." That's defensible, it compounds, and it's exactly where the money is moving.

**The takeaway:** AI didn't kill craft. It deleted the busywork around craft and left the craft fully exposed, with a much bigger price tag on it.
