Founders stare at the wrong dashboard. The numbers that go up and to the right and feel *great* are usually the ones that tell you nothing. Here are the three that actually reveal product-market fit, and the three that quietly fool you.

## The 3 that tell the truth

**1. The shape of your retention curve.** Cohort your users by signup week and plot what % are still active at week 1, 2, 4, 8. If the curve keeps sliding toward zero, you don't have PMF — full stop. If it *flattens* at some non-trivial percentage (a stable group keeps coming back), you have a product some people genuinely need. The flattening is the signal. Everything else is noise.

**2. The percentage of growth that's organic.** What share of new users came from word of mouth, not from you pushing? When people who weren't asked start telling other people, that's the market pulling the product out of your hands. A high organic % is the closest thing to a PMF heartbeat.

**3. The "how disappointed" score.** Ask active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this?" Sean Ellis's benchmark: if **40%+** say "very disappointed," you're in PMF territory. Below that, you're still searching. It's a survey, it's cheap, and it's brutally clarifying.

## The 3 that lie

**1. Total signups / registered users.** A cumulative number that only ever goes up. It feels like growth and measures nothing about whether anyone *stays*. You can have 50,000 signups and zero product-market fit.

**2. Pageviews and traffic.** Easy to inflate, emotionally satisfying, almost disconnected from value. A viral post spikes traffic and changes nothing about your business if no one sticks.

**3. Feature count / "engagement" totals.** Shipping more and counting raw clicks feels productive. But more features and more clicks often mean *more confusion*, not more value. Activity isn't the same as need.

## The reframe

PMF isn't "are people showing up?" It's "do the people who show up *come back on their own?*" Retention, organic pull, and disappointment-if-gone all measure that one thing from different angles. The vanity metrics all measure arrival, which is the easy part.

**The takeaway:** if your retention curve flattens and 40% would be very disappointed to lose you, you have something real. If not, no amount of signups will save you — go find the wedge.
